Project Description

To identify pathology in a tissue and the effects of treatment, it is essential to be able to produce accurate quantitative information about the tissue architecture and behaviour. This is not a trivial task due to the complexity and variability of real tissues. Most tissues and organs have a complex 3D organisation, which contrasts with the majority of in vitro studies on 2D cultures, which do not accurately reflect reality. This project will utilise a model of developing human neuroepithelia, specifically human brain organoids, where cells adopt their features within a developing 3D organ, to study tissue architecture. It will build upon an existing computational pipeline in Bulgakova’s lab to further computational approaches for identifying patterns and changes in their architecture, leveraging a large collection of human brain organoid images available in Palacios’ lab.

The student will use MATLAB to characterise the architecture of human brain organoids, with a focus on the number, distribution, and position of dividing cells, as the correct regulation of cell division is essential for normal brain development. The computational approach will combine predictive analytics with machine learning (ML), including traditional and deep ML. The project will begin by examining Z-sections of fixed images, then proceed to a 3D reconstruction of those sections.

The student will compare the normal human brain organoids to those that are either genetically modified using CRISPR or treated with drugs broadly used in clinics. This will both validate the developed approach and test its efficacy in identifying changes to tissue architecture and phenotype.

B2003: Single-cell transcriptomics and epigenomics applied to the evolution and development of animal embryos and larvae

❌ MSc AI in Biosciences✅ MSc Bioinformatics
  • Primary SupervisorChema Martin chema.martin@qmul.ac.uk
  • School/InstituteSchool of Biological and Behavioural Science
  • Research Pagehttps://www.martinduranlab.com
  • Positions Available2

Project Description

Animals can have direct or indirect development based on whether they lack or form a transitory life stage called larva between embryogenesis and adulthood, respectively. How larvae evolved is, however, contentious because the mechanisms controlling their development and diversification remain elusive.

Recently, my lab has proposed a new mechanism to explain the evolution of direct and indirect development by which temporal shifts (i.e., heterochrony) in trunk growth produce different larvae and life cycle transitions. While taxa with direct development and non-feeding larvae form the trunk during embryogenesis at the same time as the head, animals with feeding larvae form the trunk much later, close to their metamorphosis. However, we do not know how these different patterns of trunk development arise and, more importantly, whether they rely on changes in the temporal unfolding of conserved developmental processes or the evolution of entirely new genetic programmes of trunk formation.

To address this question, we have compared the development of two annelid larvae with different timings of trunk development. We have found that the Hox genes, a conserved gene family expressed in the trunk of nearly all bilaterian animals, are active at different times but might be controlled by the same genes in both species. This suggests that changes in the timing of activity of conserved genes cause the heterochronies in trunk formation in annelid larvae. However, to test this hypothesis and reveal the developmental and evolutionary principles facilitating trunk heterochronies in animals, we need to dissect how this body region develops in different larvae and identify the genes that initiate this process.

In this project, you will use time courses of single-cell transcriptomics and diverse bulk epigenomic data (ATAC-seq, CUT&Tag and Micro-C datasets) to explore the dynamics of trunk formation in two annelid species with distinct larval forms and timings of trunk development. You will learn how to analyse and process these datasets and perform temporal and cross-species comparisons. You will use the high-performance computing cluster Apocrita, expand your coding skills in Python and R, and learn how to visualise and present big data analyses in an engaging and clear manner.

My lab, the Martin Duran lab, is an international and welcoming team of seven researchers (3 postdocs, 3 PhD students, and one computational scientist; four female, three male). During your project, you will have extensive training and mentoring (with weekly meetings and regular informal chats via online messaging), as well as opportunities to present your work and gain feedback in our weekly lab meetings.

Project Description

Decision-making is an essential hallmark of life. At the cellular level, decision-making is controlled by gene expression that regulates the conversion of genetic information into a phenotypic output. It allows cells to determine their fate and shape their surroundings. Gene expression is surprisingly variable even between genetically identical cells and can lead to divergent behaviour of individuals within a cell population. This is particularly relevant for the survival of bacteria which are constantly exposed to fluctuating and stressful environments. Cell-to-cell variability may increase overall population fitness by enabling specialisation and division of labour to improve efficiency as well as bet-hedging by spreading the risk among individuals of being unprepared for sudden environmental change.

This interdisciplinary MSc project combines molecular cell biology with state-of-the-art biochemical imaging and advanced data analysis to reveal the physiological states of single cells within complex bacterial populations under antibiotic stress.

We have already developed statistical analysis methods to reveal the cellular diversity across bacterial populations, but we see great promise in using more advanced data science methods to pull out more nuanced insights from our data. Hence, the focus of this project will be to apply methods such cluster analysis, dimensionality reduction, machine learning and AI to map divergent behaviours of individual cells. The project will provide a new context in which to understand the fundamental biological processes that shape the life cycle of bacterial cells and enable us to better predict the cellular dynamics of bacteria in response to antibiotic treatment. This knowledge will be essential when tackling the challenges associated with bacteria, may they be societal (food security), commercial (pharmaceutical production), or clinical (antimicrobial resistance).

Project Description

Recent advances in monitoring technology have greatly improved our capacity to detect changes in insect populations. Detecting these signals in real time would enable timely and targeted interventions to mitigate biodiversity loss. A promising target for identifying stress signals exists in the morphology and condition of insect wings and faces, which convey a wealth of information about insect health and development. B2006: Studying the rules of cooperative turn taking using DeepLabCut

✅ MSc AI in Biosciences✅ MSc Bioinformatics
  • Primary SupervisorAlexander Mielke a.mielke@qmul.ac.uk
  • School/InstituteSchool of Biological and Behavioural Science
  • Research Pagehttps://www.seresearch.qmul.ac.uk/cbb/people/amielke/
  • Positions Available2

Project Description

Social interactions are an intricate dance of actions and reactions - while we know this to be true for humans, it also holds for other species who engage in daily affiliative and agonistic interactions with conspecifics. Our understanding of the complexities of these interactions has always been limited by data collection restrictions: back in the day, researchers would assign broad categories to behaviours (e.g., 'grooming', 'fight', 'play') so they could be collected using pen-and-paper, removing all qualitative information about how the interaction took place. The advent of large video datasets of interactions across species was greeted with excitement, but that soured when people realised that those videos needed to be coded. AI was meant to save everyone, but when multi-animal tracking became available, researchers reverted back to broad categorisations to identify these in large data streams. We are not much closer to using AI to ask new questions about social interactions than we were a decade ago - we can reliably find aggression in videos, but we still lump all aggressive interactions into the same category. Social grooming in primates is probably the most intensely studied cooperative behaviour, but only with regard to who grooms whom in a primate group - a grooming bout is a grooming bout. However, when observing primates, it is clear that this is not the case for them. There are, for example, body parts that are reserved for certain partners; grooming speed and turn taking rules differ; and the process is interspersed with gestures, postural changes, and breaks that make every bout unique. These processes are too fine-grained for manual coding at a large scale, but ideal for machine learning applications because multi-animal pose estimation with software like DeepLabCut now comes with pretrained models that would allow researchers to understand behaviour at the finest scale without large, trained video datasets for the species in question. I have around 1,500 videos of sooty mangabeys (Cercocebus atys atys) grooming each other, mostly in pairs. All videos are known to contain grooming and pretrained DeepLabCut models can track the individuals in the videos, so the challenge is to make sense of the resulting data. Students will choose one social parameter of grooming interactions (e.g., who grooms, which body part is being groomed, when do breaks occur) and build analytical solutions that can capture this information in pose estimation data. You will learn about deep learning applications to animal tracking and pose estimation, about social interactions and cooperation, primate behaviour, and communication. This is proof-of-concept work - this would be the first project I am aware of that uses deep learning to extract valuable qualitative information about a social interaction type in primates.

This project gives students the opportunity to apply and extend the system to tackle real problems in bioscience evidence synthesis. It combines practical machine-learning engineering (prompt design, benchmarking, data evaluation) with ecological and biological insight, and can be flexibly tailored to the student’s interests and background.

Option 1 – Building the “Living” MetaBeeAI Pipeline Transform the existing pipeline, benchmarked on bee–pesticide literature, into a living evidence synthesis system that automatically ingests new research articles as they are published. ---

Key activities: - Test prompt-engineering strategies and context-window optimisation - Compare different model families (e.g. GPT-4 vs open-weight LLMs) - Develop quantitative metrics for evaluating performance, such as faithfulness, contextual precision, and recall

---

Expected outcomes - A functional, modular LLM pipeline for automated literature extraction and synthesis - Benchmarked results demonstrating accuracy, recall, and interpretability - A cleaned dataset suitable for downstream ecological or biomedical modelling

---

Skills and impact

This project offers hands-on experience at the frontier of AI-for-Science, combining natural-language processing, data engineering, and bioscience domain expertise. One of the most surprising discoveries of the chromosome-level genome era is that many animals have kept their ancestral chromosome structures for more than 600 million years. Project Description

Electroactive bacteria (EAB) are microorganisms with the unique ability to "breathe" solid-state redox-active surfaces, transferring electrons to and from external electrodes. B2012: Brain-Like Wiring and Modelling Predictive Information

❌ MSc AI in Biosciences✅ MSc Bioinformatics

Project Description

Neural systems, from fruit fly to human, are information-processing networks that are primarily designed to predict the environment. We will study how network wiring and dynamics shape predictive information in artificial neural networks that respect key features of the brain wiring diagram (the connectome—i.e., which neurons connect to which).

We quantify information using Partial Information Decomposition (PID): a mathematical technique that splits the information two inputs carry about a target into (i) redundant (overlapping) parts, (ii) unique parts, and (iii) synergistic parts (information present only when inputs are combined). We will use practical PID measures such as our recently published ΦID measure (Mediano et al, 2025, PNAS) to track how synergy changes as networks learn to predict the next item in a sequence.

Our prior empirical work suggests that synergy grows with better prediction and increased synergy relates to increased consciousness and complex cognition in humans (Luppi et al 2022, Nature Neuroscience). Here we ask: which parameter settings of the model best reflect the synergistic information processing we observe in a range of animal species.

Students will (i) build simple spiking models, based closely on human and fruit-fly connectome graphs, (ii) compute PID (ΦID/O-information) over time-lagged variables, and (iii) map a “phase diagram” of synergy (a chart showing where different parameter regimes produce low vs. high synergy). We will also test whether the effect strengthens with network size. This research will greatly inform how network structure supports optimal learning, and even consciousness, in biological and artificial systems.

Students will be expected to have a solid coding knowledge, particularly in python. In addition, some mathematical background would be desirable, but not required.

Project Description

Project Goals The aims of this project are: 1) to learn how to develop an epigenetic clock from DNA methylation values of single CpG sites using machine learning methodologies. 2) to determine the best internal validation method that can optimise the predictive performance of an epigenetic clock.

Background Age is considered a major risk factor for many morbidities(1). However, significant heterogeneity has been observed when it comes to associating chronological age with health progression(2,3). This has led to the development of biological age predictors to predict disease risk, and those among them which are DNA methylation-based are known as “epigenetic clocks”(4). However, the performance of these clocks can be either overestimated or underestimated depending on the internal validation (split-sample, cross-validation etc) used. While different types of internal validation techniques are used among different studies to build clocks, there is no consensus about which one can produce the most accurate models. This project aims to investigate the effect of different internal validation methods using the publicly available epigenomic datasets that were used in the original epigenetic clocks’ development. Testing how the different internal validation methods will affect the supervised machine learning models’ efficiency will give us insight into the most appropriate bioinformatic approach for future studies aiming to the development of novel epigenetic clock creation.

Methodology • Search in public databases for the epigenomic datasets used in the original epigenetic clocks’ (Horvath et al, Hannum et al, Levine et al, etc) development. • Preprocessing of raw data in order to attain all relevant features (DNA methylation levels). • Feature selection and machine learning predictor algorithms development using several internal validation methods (split-sample, cross-validation, bootstrapping etc) • Assessment of the optimal internal validation method based on the performance of the machine learning predictor algorithms.

Project Description

1. Background & Rationale Sjögren’s disease (SjD) is an autoimmune disorder characterised by chronic inflammation and destruction of salivary glands. Bulk RNA-seq studies have defined global dysregulated pathways, while histology provides detailed spatial information about glandular damage—yet these two information layers are rarely integrated. The ProtoPathway concept seeks to bridge global and local biology: 1. Identify dysregulated pathways from bulk RNA-seq, and 2. Map these pathways onto tissue microarchitecture inferred from histology. This MSc project will apply a simplified ProtoPathway pipeline to an already-processed dataset of salivary gland histology images paired with matched bulk RNA-seq from SjD patients, enabling a focused exploration of glandular pathology.

2. Project Aim To implement and evaluate the ProtoPathway analysis pipeline, integrating bulk RNA-seq with quantitative histological features to identify compartment-specific or pathology-associated molecular pathways in Sjögren’s disease.

3. Objectives 1. Histology Image Feature Extraction - Use digital histology analysis (e.g., CellProfiler, QuPath, or Python-based pipelines) to quantify acinar area, ductal structures, immune infiltrates, stromal expansion, or fibrosis. - Optionally apply shallow machine-learning or clustering to derive histology-based “microenvironment states.” 2. Bulk RNA-seq Pathway Analysis - Perform differential expression (SjD vs controls) and pathway/Module enrichment (GO, KEGG, WGCNA, etc). - Identify top disease-associated pathways for subsequent mapping. 3. ProtoPathway Integration - Test correlations between histology-derived features and RNA-seq pathway scores (e.g., GSVA, ssGSEA). - Determine whether particular structural changes (e.g., immune foci density, acinar atrophy, stromal expansion) link to specific dysregulated pathways (e.g., interferon signalling, ECM/fibrosis, epithelial stress). - Visualise integrated results using heatmaps, correlation networks, and “pathology × pathway” matrices. 4. Clinical Interpretation (with Rheumatology Team) - Assess how integrated signatures correspond to clinical metadata (focus score, serology, symptom severity). - Identify candidate pathology-linked pathways that may indicate mechanistic drivers of gland dysfunction. 5. Deliverables - A modular ProtoPathway analysis script/notebook. - Summary figures linking morphological features to transcriptomic pathways. - A manuscript for publication.

4. Methods - H&E Image Processing: colour normalisation, segmentation of acini/ducts/inflammatory foci, extraction of quantitative morphological metrics. - Transcriptomic Analysis: DESeq2 or edgeR; pathway analysis via fgsea / clusterProfiler; GSVA for per-sample pathway scores. - Data Integration: correlation analysis, PCA/UMAP of integrated features, hierarchical clustering to define histology–molecular subgroups. - Visualisation: Python or R (Seaborn, ggplot2, ComplexHeatmap).

5. Skills Required Students will need basic Python or R programming, familiarity with bioinformatics pipelines, competence in RNA-seq differential expression and enrichment analysis, foundational digital histology / image analysis skills, and the ability to communicate findings effectively with clinical collaborators.
